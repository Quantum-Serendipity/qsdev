# Presentation Patterns & Anti-Patterns (Neal Ford)
- **Source**: https://nealford.com/memeagora/2013/10/16/presentation_patterns_origins.html
- **Retrieved**: 2026-05-15

## Anti-Patterns Identified

**Bullet-riddled Corpse**
A slide filled with dense text and bullet points. Ford describes seeing "long lists of bullets" as "stultifying" to audiences, representing an ineffective approach to visual communication.

**Floodmarks**
Excessive branding elements cluttering slides — company logos and conference badges taking up valuable space without adding content value.

**Dead Demo**
Live demonstrations that undermine rather than enhance presentations. According to Ford, this pattern involves "showing something that only made sense within a tool" and "spending lots of time getting everything set up at the expense of real content." The focus becomes the presenter's tool proficiency rather than audience learning.

**InfoDeck**
Slides designed primarily for reading independently, lacking the strategic use of animations and transitions that create temporal engagement during live presentations.

**Defy Defaults**
Using standard presentation templates without customization, described as "a bad idea" that fails to create distinctive visual communication.

## Patterns Recommended

**Live Demo**
Effective in specific contexts like classroom technical training where participants must master tools. Context-dependent — works when "students were required to use the tool for exercises."

**Know Your Audience**
Understanding presentation context determines appropriate technique; conference settings differ fundamentally from training rooms in objectives and information density requirements.

**Context Keeper**
Maintains audience orientation throughout presentations, using visual and narrative consistency.

**Unifying Visual Theme**
Each presentation employs consistent visual design "designed to compliment and amplify the subject rather than distract from it."

## Key Recommendations

Ford emphasizes three critical success factors:

1. **Seek feedback actively** through conference evaluations to identify what resonates
2. **Prioritize information density** over live tool demonstrations in conference settings
3. **Invest in visual design literacy**, citing influences like Presentation Zen and Slide:ology

The fundamental shift: moving from presenter-centric ego displays to audience-centric information transfer.
