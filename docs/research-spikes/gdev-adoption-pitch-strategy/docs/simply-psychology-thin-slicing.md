# Thin-Slicing Judgments in Psychology

- **Source**: https://www.simplypsychology.org/thin-slicing-psychology.html
- **Retrieved**: 2026-05-15

---

## Definition and Core Concept

Thin-slicing refers to "the ability to make accurate judgments about people or situations based on very limited information, often within a few seconds or minutes." This process involves drawing swift conclusions from minimal experiential data while relying on nonverbal cues, facial expressions, body language, and tone of voice.

## The Ambady and Rosenthal (1992) Meta-Analysis

**Research Design:**
The landmark study examined 38 studies meeting specific criteria: observations lasting under 300 seconds with ratings compared to external outcomes. Research spanned clinical, social, and deception domains, with a median of 32 participants per study and 37 raters per study.

**Key Findings:**
- Average effect size was r=.39, indicating "judges could predict outcomes from thin slices at levels significantly above chance"
- Accuracy remained consistent regardless of exposure duration (under 30 seconds versus up to 5 minutes showed no significant differences)
- Study type (laboratory versus field) and outcome type didn't substantially affect accuracy
- Nonverbal-only ratings proved equally accurate as those including verbal information

## Practical Examples

Common thin-slicing scenarios include sensing untrustworthiness from brief interactions, detecting anger through vocal tone, and forming immediate reactions to someone's appearance.

## Strengths and Limitations

**Strengths:** Systematic meta-analytic methodology enabling generalization

**Limitations:** Potential publication bias and ambiguity surrounding "objective" criterion definitions

## Implications

Findings suggest expressive behaviors efficiently communicate substantial interpersonal information, offering practical benefits for research, personnel selection, and interpersonal evaluation contexts.
