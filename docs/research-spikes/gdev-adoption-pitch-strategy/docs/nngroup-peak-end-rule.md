# The Peak-End Rule: How User Experiences Become Lasting Memories

## Psychological Foundation

The peak-end rule originates from research by Daniel Kahneman and Barbara Fredrickson (1993). Their landmark cold-water study demonstrated that memory doesn't function as an accurate recording device. Participants underwent three rounds of holding their hands in cold water at varying temperatures and durations.

The critical finding: when Round 2 ended with 30 seconds of slightly warmer water (rather than continuing at the colder temperature), 80% of participants preferred repeating that condition despite its longer total duration. "A small improvement near the end of an experience radically shifted people's perception of that event."

**The peak-end rule definition:** "A cognitive bias that impacts how people remember past events. Intense positive or negative moments (the 'peaks') and the final moments of an experience (the 'end') are heavily weighted in our mental calculus."

## UX Design Applications

### Creating Positive Peaks

Designers should identify emotionally rewarding moments in user journeys and enhance them. Duolingo exemplifies this approach by using playful visuals and encouraging feedback when users succeed.

### Addressing Negative Peaks

Difficult navigation or frustrating experiences have outsized impact on overall perception. Since "online your users have many options available to them," frustrating experiences drive users away disproportionately.

### The Power of Endings

Final impressions disproportionately shape overall satisfaction. TurboTax successfully celebrates completion with celebratory illustrations, while "please-don't-go" pop-ups actively damage user perception.

### Error States and Disruptions

When technical failures occur, clear communication matters immensely. Vague error messages create negative peaks; explaining problems and offering timelines creates trust.

## Core Design Principle

Memory operates through "snapshots rather than a complete catalogue of events," making intensity and conclusions disproportionately influential in shaping lasting impressions.
