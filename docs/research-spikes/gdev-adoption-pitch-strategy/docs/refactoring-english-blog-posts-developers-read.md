## Core Advice: The Five Key Principles

### 1. Get to the Point Immediately

The biggest mistake: "meandering" with unnecessary backstory before revealing insight.

Developers arrive with two critical questions:
1. Is this article written for someone like me?
2. How will reading this benefit me?

**You have the title plus three sentences to answer both questions.** If you haven't addressed them by paragraph two, readers abandon your post.

To signal relevance, mention familiar topics and use recognized terminology early. Unfamiliar jargon makes readers assume the article wasn't written for them.

**Example:** "There's an excellent Go testing pattern that too few people know. I can teach it to you in 30 seconds." — Immediately identifies audience (Go developers) and benefit (learning quickly).

### 2. Think One Degree Bigger

Most bloggers never ask whether their audience could expand beyond initial assumption. A post targeting "intermediate to advanced Java developers" could reach "all Java developers" with minimal changes — typically just "an extra sentence or two." This expansion could increase readership by "an order of magnitude."

### 3. Plan the Route to Your Readers

Before writing, ask: "How will people find this?" Give your post "multiple chances to succeed" rather than betting on one discovery path.

### 4. Show More Pictures

"The biggest bang-for-your-buck change you can make to a blog post is adding pictures." Break up text walls with screenshots, graphs, diagrams.

### 5. Accommodate Skimmers

Many readers skim first to decide whether full reading is worthwhile. Your article should intrigue skimmers viewing only headings and images.

## The Underlying Problem: "Why Should I Care?"

Developers instinctively ask whether content serves them. Without clear, immediate answers about relevance and benefit, attention evaporates instantly — not from disinterest in quality, but from overwhelming availability of alternatives demanding less cognitive effort to evaluate.
