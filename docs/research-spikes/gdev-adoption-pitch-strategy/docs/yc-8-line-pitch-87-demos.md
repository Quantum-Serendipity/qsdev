## Core Framework

Burak Buyukdemir analyzed successful Y Combinator pitches and identified eight essential elements every winning pitch contains:

1. **WHAT** — One-sentence company description using "[Category] for [customer] that [outcome]"
2. **PROBLEM** — Quantified pain with specific numbers rather than feelings
3. **FAILURE** — Why current solutions don't work; name the broken mechanism
4. **SOLUTION** — One-sentence mechanism description (not technology specs)
5. **WHY NOW** — What changed to enable this (regulatory, technological, or behavioral shifts)
6. **PROOF** — Traction metrics with specific timeframes
7. **TEAM** — Credentials demonstrating founder-market fit
8. **VISION** — Expansion path from initial wedge to larger market

## Three Ordering Patterns

**Numbers-First (67% of successful pitches):** Lead with strongest proof when you have revenue, contracts, or user growth.

**Insight-First (24%):** Begin with surprising discovery when you've identified something others missed.

**Magic-First (9%):** Open with impressive demo under 15 seconds (rarely recommended).

## 10 Hook Patterns

Effective opening lines include: X-for-Y comparisons, Role Replacement ("AI [role] for [industry]"), First [Category], Operating System framing, "[X] Without [Y]" constructions, Big Company Killer positioning, "$[X]B Market, [Y]% Broken," technical achievements, counterintuitive facts, and origin stories.

## Compression Principle

"Every sentence should do work." Replace vague statements with specificity. "22 words that say almost nothing" should become "twelve words" conveying category, customer, and traction.

## Common Mistakes

- Starting with the problem instead of proof
- Vague hooks lacking customer or category specificity
- Absent quantified metrics
- Buzzword solutions instead of clear mechanisms
- Generic team descriptions without specific accomplishments
