# Developer Advocacy Handbook: Talk Delivery Tips

## Core Philosophy

"The start of any great presentation is excitement about the subject matter" — without genuine excitement, your presentation loses its power.

## Pacing and Time Management

**The Speed Challenge**
Presenters naturally speak faster on stage due to adrenaline. This is normal and actually makes you more relatable. However, as you gain experience, this tendency can become problematic, leading to overstuffed talks.

**The "Less is More" Principle**
Start with a single central message. Rather than cramming content, focus on what you want your audience to remember. "Shorter is better" when considering how people consume educational content.

## Audience-Centered Design

Your presentation is one among many conference talks. Your role is to inspire deeper learning, not to demonstrate expertise. "Your job as a presenter is to make a topic interesting for people to try it out themselves. You open the door, you lead the way, and then they can spread out."

Key considerations:
- People won't retain your entire talk — plant memorable seeds instead
- Your audience doesn't need information; they need perspective and application insights
- Recordings will be watched later, so clarity and brevity matter

## Live Coding: Risks and Alternatives

**Problems with Live Demos:**
- Technical failures are inevitable (connectivity, typos, forgotten credentials)
- Code readability suffers due to projector resolution and font size requirements
- Autocomplete and browser history can expose embarrassing information
- Debugging on stage breaks narrative flow and consumes precious time
- The audience may focus on your editor/terminal setup rather than content

**Better Approach:**
Use pre-recorded screencasts with live commentary instead. This allows you to control pacing, speed up tedious compilation time, and maintain focus on your narrative while audiences can follow along in recordings.

## Managing Questions

Avoid taking questions during your presentation. While interactive, they:
- Distract from your planned narrative arc
- Create awkward moments for video recordings
- Often address topics you'll cover later or unrelated subjects

Instead, announce availability for questions afterward and clearly communicate how the audience can reach you.

## Content to Remove

**Cut these slides:**
- Agenda slides (they signal when audience can tune out)
- Corporate branding and company introductions
- Lengthy personal biographies
- Dated jokes and memes (they age poorly)

## Content to Add (Time Fillers)

**Valuable additions:**
- Slide showing where to access your presentation
- Contact information and preferred communication channels
- Colleague recommendations for follow-up expertise
- Links to your team's published resources
- Participation opportunities (repositories, mailing lists, test suites)
- Sources that informed your research

## Resource Delegation Strategy

Point audiences toward external resources rather than explaining everything yourself:

- **Comparison sites** showing current technology status
- **Deep-dive talks** by other experts on related topics
- **Test suites** letting audiences verify claims independently
- **Interactive, forked resources** (CodePen, GitHub) they can extend
- **Official specifications** adding credibility to your points
- **Curated link lists** consolidating all references

## Final Structure

Plan around one central theme with a supporting story arc. Build in flexibility so if you receive less speaking time, you can remove sections while maintaining your core message. "Offer longer Q&A if you need to," essentially using audience dialogue as a time-adjustment tool.
