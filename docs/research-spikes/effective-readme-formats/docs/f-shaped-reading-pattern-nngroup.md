# F-Shaped Pattern of Reading on the Web: Misunderstood, But Still Relevant

- **Source URL**: https://www.nngroup.com/articles/f-shaped-pattern-reading-web-content/
- **Retrieved**: 2026-05-15

---

## What Is the F-Shaped Pattern?

Nielsen Norman Group discovered this eye-tracking behavior in 2006. The pattern consists of three components:

1. **Horizontal scan** across the upper content area (top bar of the F)
2. **Second horizontal movement** down the page, covering shorter distance (lower bar)
3. **Vertical scan** along the left side (stem of the F)

Key implications: "First lines of text on a page receive more gazes than subsequent lines" and initial words on each line get more fixations than words toward the right.

## When & Why It Occurs

The F-pattern emerges when three conditions align:
- Text lacks web formatting (no bolding, bullets, subheadings)
- Users prioritize efficiency
- Users lack strong interest in reading everything

Users naturally "minimize interaction cost and maximize benefit," meaning they scan quickly with minimal effort rather than read comprehensively.

## Additional Scanning Patterns

The research identified five alternatives:
- **Layer-cake**: Scanning headings while skipping body text
- **Spotted**: Searching for specific elements like links or numbers
- **Marking**: Eyes stay fixed while content scrolls (mobile-dominant)
- **Bypassing**: Skipping repeated opening words in lists
- **Commitment**: Reading nearly everything when highly motivated

## Why It's Problematic

F-shaped scanning causes users to miss substantial content based purely on text flow. This pattern persists on both desktop and mobile devices, including right-to-left languages (mirrored F-pattern in Arabic).

## Design Solutions

Eight recommendations combat this pattern:

1. Place critical information in opening paragraphs
2. Use prominent headings and subheadings
3. Lead headings with information-rich keywords
4. Visually group related content through borders/backgrounds
5. Bold important words and phrases
6. Format links meaningfully (avoid generic "click here")
7. Apply bullets and numbered lists
8. Remove unnecessary content

The core strategy: "Do the work for the users instead of forcing them to exert effort."
