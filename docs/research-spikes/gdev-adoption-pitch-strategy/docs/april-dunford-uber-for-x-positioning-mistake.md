# When Describing Your Startup as "Uber for X" Is a Big Mistake — April Dunford

- **Retrieved**: 2026-05-15

## Core Thesis
Using "Uber for X" comparisons to describe startups is often ineffective and potentially harmful, despite their popularity among founders pitching to investors.

## Five Key Problems Identified

**1. Audience Mismatch**
The pitch works differently depending on whether you're addressing VCs or customers. Investor pitches emphasizing growth potential differ from customer pitches focused on practical value. Dunford notes "Kitchen appliance rental" communicates more clearly to prospects than "Uber for kitchen appliances."

**2. Assumed Familiarity**
Not all audiences know the comparison company. Dunford references buyers—such as CEOs of small manufacturing plants—who may never have used LinkedIn or understand its significance, making these analogies ineffective.

**3. Unintended Baggage**
Comparisons import both positive and negative associations. Comparing to Uber conjures not just efficiency but also regulatory problems, aggressive tactics, and safety concerns. Dunford's Shuddle example illustrates this: positioning as "Uber for Kids" alienates parents who associate Uber with those controversies. The company's actual messaging—"Scheduled rides for busy parents"—works better.

**4. Clarity Issues**
The comparison may obscure rather than clarify the actual business model. Dunford questions: "Airbnb for shipping packages" confuses, while PiggyBee's description—"shipping packages with travellers"—clarifies the concept better.

**5. Reduced Differentiation**
Overuse dilutes impact. Bessemer Venture Partners' Jeremy Levine noted the phrase is "no longer quite so inspiring" and should trigger "nagging reservations" about uniqueness.

## Recommended Approach
The high-concept pitch isn't positioning itself. Substantive work requires defining target segments, market position, key value propositions, and competitive differentiation.
