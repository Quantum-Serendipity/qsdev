## Overview

April Dunford defines positioning as how "your product is a leader at delivering something that a well-defined set of customers cares a lot about." It's fundamentally about context-setting—similar to an opening scene in a film that orients the audience before the story unfolds.

## What Positioning Is NOT

Positioning differs from:
- Messaging or taglines
- Brand storytelling
- Company vision or "why"
- General marketing activities

## The 5 Core Components

1. **Competitive alternatives** - What customers would do without your solution
2. **Differentiated features/capabilities** - Unique attributes versus alternatives
3. **Value for customers** - The "so what" behind those capabilities
4. **Target customer segmentation** - Specific buyers who care most about your value
5. **Market category** - The context positioning your product for success

## The Methodology: Sequential Flow

Rather than treating components independently, they interconnect:

**Start with competitive alternatives** → identify unique attributes → determine resulting customer value → segment to best-fit customers → select market category that makes your value obvious

## Real-World Example: Janna Systems

A struggling enterprise CRM competing against industry leader Siebel discovered:
- **Differentiator**: Unique relationship modeling between people across organizations
- **Best-fit customers**: Investment banks needing interpersonal insights for sales
- **Repositioning**: From "enterprise CRM" to "CRM for Investment Banks"
- **Result**: Revenue jumped from under $2M to nearly $80M in 18 months

## Three Critical Traps to Avoid

**Trap 1**: Treating all possible competitors equally rather than focusing on what customers actually consider as alternatives

**Trap 2**: Creating "phantom competitors"—theoretical threats you never encounter in actual deals. This dilutes positioning unnecessarily.

**Trap 3**: Assuming new category creation is necessary. Statistics show "90% of tech companies that have gone public over the past five years have been positioned in existing markets rather than creating new ones."

## The Positioning Statement Limitation

Traditional fill-in-the-blank positioning statements assume single correct answers. In reality, most products could occupy multiple viable positions depending on customer segment and market context. This rigid approach provides limited practical value.

## Key Insight from Practice

Good positioning creates accurate customer assumptions about competition, features, target buyers, and pricing. Bad positioning generates false expectations, forcing sales and marketing teams to undo damage before closing deals.
