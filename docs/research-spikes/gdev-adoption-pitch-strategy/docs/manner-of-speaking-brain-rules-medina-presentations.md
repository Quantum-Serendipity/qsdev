# John Medina's Brain Rules for Presentations
- **Source**: https://mannerofspeaking.org/2015/07/03/a-brain-hack-for-your-next-presentation/
- **Retrieved**: 2026-05-15

## The 10-Minute Attention Cycle

According to Medina's research, audience attention typically declines after approximately 10 minutes of continuous presentation. In a standard one-hour talk, most listeners have disengaged within 15 minutes. This occurs because "the brain needs regular breaks for it to digest new information efficiently."

## Presentation Structure: 10-Minute Segments

Medina divides presentations into 10-minute modules with this approach:

**Each segment begins with a general concept**, described as "always large, always general, always filled with 'gist,' and always explainable in one minute." The remaining nine minutes develop the core concept with supporting details.

This method leverages how cognition operates: "the brain processes meaning before detail." Starting with overarching ideas enables better comprehension than beginning with specifics.

## The "Hook" System: Resetting Attention

At the 10-minute mark, speakers must introduce compelling, emotionally resonant material that serves as a "hook." These hooks must follow three principles:

1. **Emotional trigger**: Fear, humor, happiness, or nostalgia effectively engage audiences
2. **Relevance**: The hook must connect meaningfully to the content; storytelling works exceptionally well
3. **Positioning**: Hooks appear between modules, either concluding or introducing segments

Effective hooks create "something that triggers an orienting response toward the speaker" without feeling forced or unrelated.

## Results and Outcomes

Medina observed that after implementing hooks, students maintained engagement through multiple cycles. Eventually, he could omit later hooks while sustaining audience focus — evidence that strategic attention management significantly improves learning retention.
