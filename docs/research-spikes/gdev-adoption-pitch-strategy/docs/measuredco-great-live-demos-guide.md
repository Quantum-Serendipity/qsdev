# How to Do Great Live Demos — and Why They're Important to Get Right

## Definition & Purpose

Live demos are real-time presentations (not pre-recorded) showcasing working products and features. "Live demos are a staple of digital teams. They're the most efficient way to show progress."

## What to Demo

Three criteria determine demo-worthiness:
1. **Visual quality** — Even abstract elements like design tokens (CSS code strings) can be shown effectively
2. **Interestingness** — Small, well-executed work is acceptable if audiences have requested it
3. **Audience understanding** — Content must be comprehensible to attendees

The authors advise: "if you don't have at least 15 minutes of stuff to show, it's not worth the time and effort." Code refactoring and discarded work typically don't qualify unless they support a broader narrative.

## Timing & Frequency

Share work early for constructive feedback. Demo weekly on multi-month projects, every few weeks on longer initiatives. Adjust schedules around organizational holidays.

## Audience & Invitations

Cast wide nets initially to reach stakeholders with tangential interest. Building regular audiences creates positive cultural momentum around demo series.

## Presentation Structure

**Presenters should work in pairs:**
- One person handles slides and context-setting
- Another conducts the actual demonstration
- Support roles include monitoring chat, tracking time, and managing technical issues

**Slide deck components:**
- Context and purpose explanation
- Agenda and roadmap
- Demo signposting
- Speaker transitions
- Roadmap of upcoming work
- Question prompts

The authors recommend: "Simple, concise slides are best, but each slide should have enough context to make sense to people who weren't there."

## Preparation

**Dry runs are essential.** Practice with trusted colleagues and gather honest feedback. Trim content to 45-60 minutes maximum. Prepare backup screenshots or screen recordings for technical contingencies.

Preparation time decreases with experience — initial demos may require 1-2 days; experienced teams need 2-3 hours.

## Technical Considerations

- Join 10 minutes early to test audio, video, and screen-sharing
- Present from quiet, well-lit locations
- Use quality cameras and microphones
- Record demos (with permission) for absent team members
- Notify participants before recording starts

## Presentation Delivery

Key performance elements include pacing and practice-based improvement. The authors note: "demos are a reasonably low-jeopardy way of developing presenting skills."

## Question Management

Decide in advance whether to allow questions during or only at the end. Reserve at least 10 minutes for final Q&A — less signals disinterest in feedback.

**Handling disruptions:** If senior stakeholders dominate discussions, politely redirect questions to the end unless they hold critical project roles.

Use chat moderation to manage distractions while allowing participation.

## Follow-Up Actions

- Save chat histories for unanswered questions
- Share slides immediately (review speaker notes first)
- Review recordings for quality assurance
- Re-share Q&A in text format
- Encourage attendees to promote future demos

The authors emphasize: "Not following up properly can undo effort you've put into demoing well."

## Building Confidence

"There's a lot to think about, but demos don't have to be scary." Performance improves substantially through repetition and established processes. Most audiences are "kind, patient and understanding" about technical hiccups.
